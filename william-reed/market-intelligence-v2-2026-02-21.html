01 — Intelligence

What Matters Today

The signals shaping strategy across food, retail and marketing this cycle. Four stories you need to act on.

Lead Signal — Regulation

CMA Green-Claims Clampdown Puts UK Food Marketers on Notice

The Competition and Markets Authority is stepping up its focus on green-claims compliance in the food sector. Sustainability messaging is being pulled into supply-chain substantiation requirements, meaning marketing teams can no longer treat environmental claims as brand storytelling alone. Any claim now needs verifiable evidence across the full supply chain, from sourcing to shelf.

Reputational Risk

Tesco, Lidl and Farmfoods Face Fresh Wave of Recall-Related Reputational Sensitivity

Multiple product recalls across three major retailers in a single cycle signal either a supply-chain quality spike or heightened regulatory scrutiny. For marketing and comms teams, the operational response now directly feeds consumer trust metrics. Brands supplying these retailers should audit their recall communication protocols immediately.

Innovation

Higgidy Expands Quiche Range with Globally Inspired Flavours

Premium convenience continues to find white space. Higgidy's expansion into globally inspired flavour profiles reflects the ongoing premiumisation trend in chilled prepared foods, where quality credentials and provenance storytelling drive margin over volume. Category managers should note the format innovation as a template for range extension.

Operations

Lidl and Waitrose Retrain Store Staff After Food-Waste Exposé

Operational behaviour is now a brand risk vector. Both retailers have committed to staff retraining programmes following media scrutiny of in-store food waste handling. For brand owners and suppliers, this signals that retailers are increasingly sensitive to operational narratives that undermine sustainability positioning. Expect supplier scorecards to incorporate waste metrics.

05 — Emerging Signals

Where the Market Is Heading

Themes generating rising coverage and conversation across industry sources. Early indicators worth tracking.

Convergence

Influencer Disclosure Compliance Is Converging Across Regulators and Platforms

The ASA's existing rules and TikTok's branded-content disclosure tooling are moving toward the same requirements. Food and drink brands using influencer partnerships should expect a single, stricter compliance standard to emerge — not two parallel regimes. Marketing teams should audit current disclosure practices against both ASA guidelines and platform-native tools now, before enforcement tightens.

Structural Shift

App-Led Value Spilling Into Non-Owned Channels

Co-op's member pricing on Just Eat signals that loyalty propositions are no longer contained within owned ecosystems. If this model works, expect other grocers to follow — fundamentally changing how delivery platforms negotiate with retailers and how consumers perceive value across channels.

Infrastructure

Retail Media Measurement Plumbing Still Being Built Out

Despite significant investment, the measurement infrastructure for retail media remains immature. Standardisation is lagging behind spend growth, creating a window where brands are allocating budget without reliable attribution. Watch for industry body intervention or a major retailer publishing transparent measurement benchmarks.

07 — Context & Analysis

The Bigger Picture

Compliance-Adjacent Marketing Risk Is the Defining Theme of This Cycle

This intelligence cycle is being shaped by compliance-adjacent marketing risk rather than blockbuster campaigns or major commercial launches. The three dominant threads — green claims governance, advertising disclosure hygiene, and recall-linked trust sensitivity — all share a common characteristic: they turn regulatory and operational activity into brand-facing risk.

For marketing directors and comms teams, the actionable insight is that compliance is no longer a legal function operating behind the brand. It is the brand. The CMA's green-claims focus, the ASA's influencer disclosure trajectory, and the reputational sensitivity around product recalls all demand that marketing teams have direct sight of compliance activity — not as a reporting line, but as a planning input.

The brands that navigate this cycle well will be those that treat regulatory awareness as a competitive advantage: moving early on disclosure, auditing supply-chain claims proactively, and building recall-response protocols that protect rather than erode consumer trust.

Less healthy food and drink advertising restrictions are now in force across all UK broadcast and online media. Brands affected should have already transitioned creative and media plans. Any remaining non-compliant activity creates regulatory exposure.

CMA Publishes Green-Claims Supply-Chain Guidance

The Competition and Markets Authority's updated guidance extends green-claims scrutiny beyond consumer-facing marketing into supply-chain substantiation. This is the regulatory foundation for today's lead intelligence on the CMA clampdown.

Ongoing

TikTok Mandates Branded-Content Disclosure Tooling

TikTok now requires branded-content disclosure through its native tooling for all commercial posts. This aligns platform enforcement with ASA expectations and creates a de facto single standard for influencer marketing compliance on the platform.

Ongoing

Retail Media “Platformisation” Continues to Accelerate

Retail media networks are maturing from ad-hoc inventory sales into programmatic platforms. Measurement, attribution and cross-retailer comparability remain the critical gaps. Brands increasing retail media spend should demand transparent performance benchmarks.
