Lead Signal — Retail Media

Co-op Dual-Screen Checkout Rollout Creates New Retail Media Surfaces

Co-op begins rolling out dual-screen checkouts across 150 UK stores by April, creating always-on in-store surfaces for member value and deal messaging. The retail-media implications for convenience are significant — these screens represent controlled moments at the point of decision, with mechanics that could extend beyond member offers into paid or partner placements.

Recall Risk

Tesco, Farmfoods and Lidl feature in fresh Food Standards Agency recall communications requiring precise copy discipline across store, web and CRM channels. The formal notice artefact — product name, pack size, dates, customer action — becomes canonical copy for everything downstream. Marketing teams adjacent to customer service should expect spikes in refund-path questions.

Particularly relevant for FMCG and retailer-adjacent buying teams building off-site audience strategies beyond walled-garden retailer data.

04 — Emerging Signals

Where the Market Is Heading

Themes generating rising coverage and conversation across industry sources. Early indicators worth tracking.

Consumer Friction

Recall Communications Framed as Awareness-Plus-Friction Problem

Recall comms are being treated as an awareness-plus-friction problem in consumer discussion. People focus on whether they notice the notice and whether refunds feel effortless. A secondary reputational wrinkle: scam and fraud chatter directed at retailer customer comms handles is muddying the channel, making legitimate recall messaging harder to land.

Structural Shift

Retailer Promo Surface Multiplication at Point of Decision

Retailers are increasing the number of promo surfaces controlled at the moment of decision. Co-op puts deal and membership messaging onto checkout screens; Morrisons’ receipt-led personalisation pushes offers into the post-purchase moment. Sainsbury’s in-store screen network keeps focus on centrally managed content delivery with contextual placement logic.

Infrastructure

Marketing Operations Framed as Stack Upgrade, Not Single Platform Bet

Marketing operations tooling is being framed as a stack upgrade rather than a single platform bet. Dept’s Adobe tie-up is about content workflow scale; Unilever’s Google Cloud work is pitched as AI-enabled measurement and commerce discovery. On the practitioner side, “explainable” measurement features are positioned to make performance narratives more defensible.

06 — Context & Analysis

The Bigger Picture

Three Structural Themes Define This Cycle

This intelligence cycle is shaped by three parallel structural themes rather than a single dominant story. First, recall notice management is becoming a canonical copy problem — the formal notice artefact (product name, pack size, dates, customer action) now drives everything downstream from store POS to contact-centre scripts to social responses. Marketing teams adjacent to customer service should expect operational spikes when notices involve short-shelf-life lines.

Second, the focus is not on one new retail media channel but a widening set of controlled moments — receipt personalisation, checkout display inventory, last-mile app pricing constructs. For brands, this raises the bar on creative versions built for context (store journey, mission, time of day) and measurement narratives connecting in-store exposure to repeat purchase.

Third, data and tooling partnerships like StackAdapt–Experian reinforce the push toward identity and activation options that function under tightening privacy constraints, particularly for FMCG advertisers operating across retailer media, social video and performance channels. The brands that win this cycle will be those that treat controlled surfaces, operational copy discipline and privacy-era data activation as integrated capabilities, not separate workstreams.

The brands that win are the ones that see what’s coming.
